# Service Suspended: Episode 3 — Story Script

## Story Summary

The player wakes in a windowless train cart — the Conductor's holding cell for "irregular passengers." The room feels like a compressed version of every commute they've ever taken: same seats, same overhead bins, same fluorescent hum, but wrong in ways that are hard to name.

As the player explores the room and rotates through its variants using tickets, they uncover lore fragments that reveal the true nature of the loop system: it is not a machine. It is not a transit project. It is something *old* — something that existed before the trains, before the stations, before the city itself. The trains and stations are just the shape it wears now. A century ago it might have been a stagecoach route that never ended. Before that, a river you couldn't stop sailing. The loop takes whatever form feels most routine, most invisible, most like something you'd never think to question.

The Conductor isn't the architect. He's a vessel — the longest-serving passenger, hollowed out and filled with purpose by the loop itself. He doesn't run the system. The system runs through him.

And the Lady in Red — the one who helped the player, who seemed so human, so sympathetic — appears in the lore across *decades* of entries. Same description. Same red coat. Same advice. She has been here longer than the Conductor. Longer than anyone. The lore doesn't explain what she is, but it makes clear: she is not a passenger. She has never been a passenger.

The player assembles a map of the loop system, solves the room's puzzles, collects golden tickets, and escapes the cart. The Lady in Red's advice still echoes — *hold onto something specific* — but by the end, the player may be starting to wonder: whose side is she actually on?

## Lore Items

Lore items are discovered in close-up interaction scenes across all three room variants. They are stored in the inventory and can be re-read at any time. Each lore item serves a dual purpose: worldbuilding and embedded puzzle clues.

### Variant A Lore

#### Crumpled Schedule
*Found: Overhead compartment (close-up scene)*

> **NORTHBOUND SERVICE — REVISED TIMETABLE**
>
> 05:15 — Platform 1 (Origin) — *Do not board before signal*
> 06:40 — Junction 7 — Transfer point
> **07:55 — Platform 9 — RESTRICTED**
> 09:10 — Terminal Loop — *Automatic return*
>
> *Handwritten at the bottom:* "The 7:55 never arrives. Don't wait for it."

*[The time 7:55 is a clue for the clock puzzle.]*

#### Passenger Logbook (Pages 1-3)
*Found: Desk drawer (close-up scene, Variant A only)*

> **Page 1:**
> I don't know who built this system. None of us do. The station was already here when the city was founded. I've seen the old survey maps — the ones from before there were roads. The tracks are on them. Not planned. Just *present.* Like rivers. Like geological features.
>
> **Page 2:**
> They built the station around the tracks because what else do you do? The city grew along the route the way cities grow along waterways. No one questioned it. You don't question a river. You just use it.
>
> **Page 3:**
> The first conductors weren't hired. They were *found* — passengers who'd been riding so long they'd become part of the operation. The transit authority listed them as employees retroactively. Gave them uniforms. Nobody remembered them boarding.

#### Torn Map Fragment #1
*Found: Behind seat cushion (close-up scene)*

*[A torn piece of paper showing part of a transit map. One corner shows "ORIGIN" with a line leading east. The torn edge cuts through a junction. The paper stock is much older than it should be — thick, fibrous, hand-pressed. The ink has a faint reddish tint.]*

#### Torn Map Fragment #2
*Found: Inside overhead luggage bag (close-up scene)*

*[Shows a section of track forming a loop, with a small "X" marked at one point. The handwriting next to it reads: "emergency access — needs power." In the margin, a different hand has written: "She showed me where to mark it."]*

### Variant B Lore

#### Stamped Tickets (Impossible Dates)
*Found: Under seat (close-up scene)*

> A bundle of three tickets, each stamped with dates:
>
> - **March 14, 1987** — Route 4-7-2 — "PASSENGER #0041"
> - **March 14, 1923** — Route 4-7-2 — "PASSENGER #0041"
> - **March 14, 1856** — Route 4-7-2 — "PASSENGER #0041"
>
> *Same route. Same passenger number. Three different centuries. The handwriting on each ticket is different, but the stamp is identical — same ink, same pressure, same machine. Or the same something.*

*[Passenger number 0041 is part of the combination lock code. Route 4-7-2 is another part.]*

#### Passenger Logbook (Pages 4-6)
*Found: Floor panel compartment (close-up scene, Variant B only)*

> **Page 4:**
> The transit authority tried to audit the system in 1971. They sent three inspectors down to the sub-platform level. Two came back. The third was found six months later, working as a conductor on a line that doesn't appear on any official map. He didn't recognize his own name.
>
> **Page 5:**
> I've been interviewing long-term passengers. They all mention a woman. Red coat. Appears when you're lost. Gives advice. Tells you to collect tickets. I asked when they first saw her. The answers span forty years. The description never changes. Not "similar." *Identical.*
>
> **Page 6:**
> I found a photograph from the 1940s in the station archives. A platform crowd. In the background, at the edge of the frame — red coat. Same face. I'm certain of it. The photograph is eighty years old. She looks exactly the same.
>
> I showed it to one of the conductors. He went pale. He said: "She was here before I was. She'll be here after."
>
> The date on my watch says March 14th. It has said March 14th for a very long time.

*[The date March 14th — written as 3/14 — is the final part of the combination lock code. The Lady in Red references plant the seed for Episode 4's reveal.]*

#### Torn Map Fragment #3
*Found: Inside floor panel (close-up scene)*

*[Shows a branching section of track. One branch is labeled "TERMINAL LOOP" and curves back on itself. The other branch leads to a small square labeled "EXIT — REQUIRES GOLDEN SERVICE." A note in the margin: "The exit was never on the official map. She says it exists. I believe her. I don't know why I believe her."]*

#### Torn Map Fragment #4
*Found: Behind loose wall panel (close-up scene, Variant B only)*

*[Shows a wiring schematic overlaid on a track section. Nodes are labeled A through F, with lines connecting them. A note reads: "Door circuit follows the same path as the emergency line. Restore the route, restore the door."]*

### Variant C Lore

#### PA System Transcript
*Found: Desk surface (close-up scene, Variant C only)*

> **[AUTOMATED TRANSCRIPT — PA SYSTEM LOG]**
>
> 05:15 — "Good morning, passengers. Service is running on schedule."
> 06:40 — "Now approaching Junction 7. Please have your tickets ready."
> 07:55 — "The **7:55 departure** has been delayed indefinitely. We apologize for the inconvenience."
> 09:10 — "Welcome back. Service is running on schedule."
> 05:15 — "Good morning, passengers. Service is running on schedule."
>
> *[The log repeats. It has been repeating. The timestamps loop. There is no end to the transcript — it continues off the edge of the page, the same five lines, over and over.]*
>
> *[At the very bottom, in handwriting that doesn't match any other note in the room:]* "It was saying this before there were speakers. Before there were trains. The words change. The announcement doesn't."

*[Confirms 7:55 as the clock puzzle solution.]*

#### Final Logbook Entry
*Found: Overhead compartment, hidden behind lining (close-up scene, Variant C only)*

> **Final Entry (unnumbered):**
>
> If you're reading this, you're in the cart. That means he caught you, which means you got close enough to catch.
>
> I spent eleven years investigating this system. I know less now than when I started. Here is what I can tell you:
>
> The loop is not a machine. It is not infrastructure. It is something that *occurs* — like weather, like gravity. The trains are just the current shape it takes. Before there were trains, there were carriages on roads that circled back. Before that, footpaths. Before that, I don't know. Animal trails, maybe. Migration routes that never ended.
>
> The Conductor is not in charge. No one is in charge. The Conductor is the passenger who has been here the longest — so long that the loop started *moving through him* instead of him moving through it. He is a function. A role the system needed filled. He told me once that he couldn't remember his name. He said it like he was reporting the weather.
>
> The golden tickets are older than the transit system. I found one in a museum archive, pressed between pages of a ledger from 1802. Same material. Same shimmer. They are the loop's own substance. Its credentials. It cannot deny what is made from itself.
>
> There is a woman in a red coat. She has been here as long as the loop. Maybe longer. Every account I've found across a hundred years of records describes her the same way. She helps. She guides. She tells you to collect the tickets.
>
> I don't know what she is. But I know she is not a passenger. Passengers decay here — they lose memories, lose themselves. She hasn't lost anything. She is *exactly* the same in every account. That is not how people work.
>
> I don't think she's malicious. But I don't think she's human, either. And I can't figure out what she gains from helping people escape. If she's been here this long and she wanted to leave, she'd have left.
>
> Unless leaving isn't what she's here for. Unless helping isn't kindness — it's *procedure.* Like she's following a protocol none of us can see.
>
> I keep coming back to something. Every account I've found — every passenger she helped — she guided them all the way to the end. And then something happened. The accounts stop. I can never find out whether they made it. Just that she was there, at the very last moment, waiting to see what they'd do.
>
> Hold onto something specific. That part of her advice is real. The loop dissolves the generic. Stay particular. Stay *you.*
>
> But be careful about who told you that, and why.
>
> — V. Marques, Independent Investigator
> (Eleven years in the system. I can still remember my daughter's name. That's how I know I haven't been here long enough.)

#### Torn Map Fragment #5
*Found: Inside seat armrest compartment (close-up scene, Variant C only)*

*[The final piece. Shows the origin station at the top and a dotted line leading to a golden square: "SERVICE EXIT." Below it: "Authorization: Golden Service Pass x3." The paper on this fragment is different from the others — older, thicker, with a faint warmth to it, like it's been sitting in sunlight that doesn't exist in this room. The fragment completes the map when combined with fragments 1-4.]*

## Puzzle Context & Clue Cross-References

### Clock Puzzle — Solution: 7:55
- **Variant A:** Crumpled Schedule lists "07:55 — Platform 9 — RESTRICTED" with handwritten note "The 7:55 never arrives."
- **Variant C:** PA System Transcript shows "The 7:55 departure has been delayed indefinitely."
- The player must cross-reference these two sources across rotations to determine the clock should be set to 7:55.
- **Reward:** A golden ticket hidden in a compartment behind the clock face.

### Combination Lock — Solution: 0041-472-314
- **Variant B:** Stamped Tickets show PASSENGER #0041 and Route 4-7-2.
- **Variant B:** Logbook Page 6 references "March 14th" as the stuck date (3/14).
- The player must assemble: Passenger number (0041) + Route (472) + Date (314).
- **Reward:** A fuse needed for the wire puzzle, plus a screwdriver.

### Jigsaw Map — 5 Fragments Across All Variants
- Fragments are found in close-up scenes across all three variants.
- The completed map reveals: the circuit layout for the wire puzzle, the existence of the exit, and that golden tickets are made from the loop's own substance.
- **Reward:** Required for the wire puzzle (shows the wiring path). Also delivers the core lore revelation about the system.

### Wire Puzzle — Final Puzzle
- **Requires:** Completed map (shows circuit path), fuse (from combination lock), screwdriver (from combination lock).
- The player connects nodes A through F following the path shown on the assembled map. The fuse must be placed, and the screwdriver is used to open the panel.
- **Reward:** The door unlocks. Escape sequence triggers.

## Escape Sequence

*[The player completes the wire puzzle. A loud mechanical CLUNK. The door shudders.]*

**PA:** "Door... [static] ...door protocol... [static]..."

*[The PA cuts out entirely. Silence for the first time in the game. No train sounds. No hum. Nothing.]*

*[The door slides open slowly. Beyond it: a narrow corridor between train carts, dimly lit. And standing there, as if she's been waiting — the Lady in Red.]*

Lady In Red: You actually did it.

You: How did you find me?

Lady In Red: I've been outside this door for a while. I wasn't sure you'd make it.

You: You said you didn't remember what you'd be going back to.

Lady In Red: I thought about it. What you said. About finding out.

*[She glances back down the corridor. A distant sound — heavy footsteps. Rhythmic. Getting closer.]*

Lady In Red: He knows the door is open. We need to move. Now.

*[The player and the Lady in Red begin moving through the corridor. The train carts blur past as they run. The footsteps behind them grow louder, heavier. The Conductor is coming.]*

**PA:** *[Crackling back to life, distorted:]* "Irregular... passengers... will be... reassigned..."

*[The corridor opens up. Light — real light, not fluorescent — spills in from ahead. A platform. A station. The train doors are open.]*

Lady In Red: The station! Go — get off!

*[They burst through the doors onto the platform. The air is different. Cold. Real. The station stretches ahead of them — turnstiles at the far end, an exit sign glowing above.]*

*[Behind them, the Conductor steps off the train. He doesn't run. He walks. Steady. Patient. He knows the station is still his territory.]*

Lady In Red: Don't stop. Don't look back. Just run.

*[The player and the Lady in Red sprint across the platform toward the turnstiles. The Conductor follows at his relentless, measured pace.]*

*[As they run, the player spots something on the ground near a bench — a transit pass, glinting with the same golden shimmer as the tickets. The player grabs it.]*

**INTERNAL MONOLOGUE:**
> A transit pass. Golden. Same material as the tickets. This is the way out.

*[They reach the turnstiles. The exit is right there. The Lady in Red stops, breathing hard. The Conductor is still approaching, unhurried.]*

*[The player looks at the pass. Looks at the turnstile. One slot. One pass.]*

*[Fade to black before the choice is made.]*

*[Text appears on screen:]*

> **"One pass. One way out."**

*[Beat.]*

> **"See you in Episode 4."**

*[Fade out. Return to main menu.]*

## Thematic Notes

### The Loop as Cosmic Occurrence
The loop is not a machine or a failed experiment. It is something ancient and perhaps fundamental — a pattern in reality itself, like a current or a tide. It takes the shape of whatever feels most routine in a given era: footpaths, carriage routes, train lines. The horror isn't that someone built a trap. The horror is that the trap is *natural.* It was always there. The city grew around it the way life grows around a hot spring — drawn to it, shaped by it, unaware of the danger.

### The Conductor as Vessel
The Conductor was once a person — a passenger who rode the loop so long that it began to move *through* him rather than him through it. He is a function, not an authority. He doesn't control the system; the system expresses itself through him. His desire for a replacement (Episode 2) is the last flicker of personhood — he's tired, but even his tiredness serves the loop's need for continuity.

### The Lady in Red — Seeds of Doubt, Seeds of Trust
Episode 3 plants ambiguity without resolution. The lore establishes:
- She appears identically across decades of records (1940s photograph, consistent descriptions spanning 40+ years)
- She does not decay the way passengers do (no memory loss, no degradation)
- She has been here longer than the Conductor — possibly longer than the loop's current form
- Her advice is *useful* — "hold onto something specific" genuinely works as a defense
- But her motivations are unknown, and the question "why hasn't she left?" has no answer
- Crucially: she *joins* the player in the escape. She runs with them. She seems to want out too.

The player should leave Episode 3 with two competing feelings: the lore says she isn't human, but the escape says she's an ally. This tension is the engine for Episode 4's final choice. The King in Yellow influence isn't that she's a monster — it's that her true nature is *unknowable* to the player at the moment they have to decide whether to trust her with everything.

The real twist (revealed only in Episode 4's good ending): she is not a victim, not a villain, and not a lure. She is an *examiner.* The loop is a test. She administers it. Her help is genuine — she gives the player every tool they need to pass. But the final test isn't a puzzle or a resource check. It's a moral one: when you have one way out and someone else needs it, what do you do? The loop doesn't test intelligence. It tests character.

### Memory as Resistance
The Lady in Red's advice — "hold onto something specific" — remains the thematic backbone. It works. It's real. And in retrospect (after Episode 4's reveal), it was never a trick: it was *preparation.* She was training the player to stay human, because staying human is the only way to pass the test. The person who holds onto their specificity — their memories, their empathy, their willingness to sacrifice — is the person who gives away the pass.

### Tickets as the Loop's Own Substance
Golden tickets aren't manufactured. They're made from the same stuff as the loop itself — its credentials, its substance. Using them to escape is like using a river's own current to swim to shore. The system can't deny what is made from itself. The transit pass found at the station is the ultimate expression of this: one final golden artifact, one slot in the turnstile, and a choice that defines everything.

## Setup for Episode 4

The player exits Episode 3 with:
- **Golden tickets** — pieces of the loop's own substance, used as resources in Episode 4's management gameplay
- **The transit pass** — a single golden pass found at the station, fits one turnstile slot, the key to the final choice
- **The Lady in Red at their side** — she escaped the cart with them, she's running too, but the lore says she's been here longer than anyone and she isn't human
- **The question** — She helped you. She ran with you. But the lore says she's been here for over a century and never left. Why is she running *now*?

### Episode 4 — Resource Management & The Final Choice

Episode 4 takes place in the station. The player and the Lady in Red must navigate through the station toward the turnstiles at the exit while the Conductor pursues them. The gameplay is resource management — spending golden tickets to unlock routes, bypass obstacles, and stay ahead of the Conductor.

The game ends at the turnstile. One pass. One slot. Two people.

### The Two Endings

**Ending A — Bad Ending: "Use the pass yourself"**

The player swipes the pass. The turnstile opens. They step through.

Fade to white.

The player wakes up in their bedroom. Morning light. Everything is normal. The alarm clock reads a reasonable time. The ceiling is intact — no crack. For a moment, it feels like freedom.

Then they see the nightstand.

A conductor's hat. And a hole punch.

The player reaches for the hat. It fits perfectly.

Fade to black. The loop has a new Conductor. The system got what it needed: someone who chose themselves. Someone who, when it mattered, was *generic* — acted out of self-preservation, the most routine impulse there is. The Lady in Red was right: the loop dissolves the generic. But it also *recruits* it.

**Ending B — Good Ending: "Give the pass to the Lady in Red"**

The player holds out the pass. The Lady in Red looks at them — really looks at them, in a way she hasn't before. She takes it. She swipes it. The turnstile opens.

She steps through.

The Conductor stops walking. He is right behind the player. The player braces.

But the Conductor doesn't grab them. He pauses. Opens a small leather book. Notes something down — precisely, mechanically. Closes the book. Turns around. Walks back toward the train. Gone.

The player stands alone at the turnstile. It shouldn't work without a pass. But it clicks open.

On the other side, the Lady in Red is standing in light that doesn't come from any fixture. Her red coat is brighter than it has ever been — not red like fabric. Red like something *alive.* She is looking at the player, and for the first time, she is not performing humanity. Her face is calm and vast and *other.*

Lady In Red: He passed.

*[She is not talking to the player. She is talking to something the player cannot see.]*

Lady In Red: *(to the player)* You held onto something specific. All the way to the end.

Lady In Red: Not everyone does.

*[She smiles. It is warm, but it is not a human smile. It is something older and larger wearing the shape of warmth.]*

Lady In Red: Go home.

Fade to white.

The player wakes up in their bedroom. Morning light. The alarm clock rings. They reach over and turn it off. The ceiling has no crack. The nightstand has no hat, no punch. Just a coffee mug and a phone with a normal notification.

They get up. They get dressed. They leave for work. The commute is unremarkable. The train arrives on time. The doors open and close. They get to the office.

Everything is normal. And for the first time, normal feels like enough.

Fade to black.

### What the Good Ending Reveals
The Lady in Red is not a passenger, not a victim, and not a villain. She is something *outside* the loop that administers it — an examiner, a cosmic proctor. The loop is a test of character, not intelligence. Every puzzle, every ticket, every piece of lore was preparation for one question: *when you can only save one person, who do you choose?*

The Conductor notes "he passed" because the Conductor is the recording mechanism — the system's clerk. The pass opens the turnstile for the Lady in Red because the test requires the player to give it away. The turnstile then opens for the player because *passing the test* is the real authorization. The golden pass was never the exit. The choice was.

The Lady in Red has been doing this for as long as the loop has existed. She helps every passenger. She gives real advice. She runs with them. She makes them care about her. And then she stands at the turnstile and waits to see what they do.

She is kind. She is not human. Both of those things are true at the same time.
